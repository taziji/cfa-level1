Alternative Investment
  • Traditional vs. Alternative investments
    • Traditional: long-only investments in stocks, bonds, and cash, etc.
    • Alternative: other investment vehicles which fall outside the scope of traditional investments, extensive use of leverage.
    • The correlation between alternative and traditional investments is low. It seems that adding alternative investments to a portfolio will improve both portfolio risk and expected return(risk/return profile).
    • Alternative investment return: leptokurtic, negatively skewed
    • VaR, Sortino ratio would be appropriate, not Sharp ratio
  • Characteristics
    • Illiquidity of underlying investments
    • Narrow manage specialization
    • Low correlation with traditional investments
    • Low level of regulation and less transparency
    • Limited and potentially problematic historical risk and return data
    • Unique legal and tax considerations
  • Hedge Fund (HF)
    • Characteristics
      • Aggressively managed investment portfolios across asset classes. Use of leverages, take long and short positions, and/or uses derivatives.
      • Aimed at higher returns, either in absolute or relative sense.
      • Private investment partnership open to a limited number of investors
      • willing and able to make a large initial investment.
    • Hedge fund indices may not reflect actual performance, since
      • Survivorship bias
      • Backfill bias
    • Restrictions on redemptions.
      • Lockup period
      • Notice period
    • Structures
      • Limited partner (LP) 出钱不出力
      • General partner (GP)
    • Funds of funds: Funds of funds are funds that hold a portfolio of hedge funds.
      • Advantage
        • FOFs enable small investors to have returns in hedge funds.
        • FOFs have some expertise in conducting due diligence on hedge funds.
        • Negotiate better redemption terms for investors.
        • FOFs invest in numerous hedge funds, diversifying across fund strategies, investment regions, and management styles.
      • Disadvantage
        • FOFs managers charge an additional layer of fees beyond the fees charged by the individual hedge funds in the portfolio.
    • HF Strategies
      • Event-driven strategies (“bottom up” strategy)
        • Merger arbitrage: long the stock of the company being acquired, and short the stock of the acquiring company.
        • Distressed/restructuring: focus on the securities of companies either in bankruptcy or perceived to be near to bankruptcy.
        • Activist shareholder: purchase sufficient equity to influence a company's policies or direction.
        • Special situations: focus on companies that are currently engaged in restructuring activities other than merger/acquisitions and bankruptcy.
      • Relative value strategies (Seek to profit from a pricing discrepancy between related securities)
      • Macro strategies (“Top-down” approach)
      • Equity hedge strategies (Focused on public equity markets)
        • Market Neutral
        • Fundamental Growth
        • Quantitative Directional
        • Short Bias
        • Sector Specific
    • HF Fees
      • Management fee
        • Based on capital under management
      • Incentive fee
        • Only earned if the return exceeds a hurdle rate 先判断hurdle rate
        • Based on profits net of (or before) management fee 再计算Base （fund begin x (return rate - hurdle rate) - Management fee) ,最后✖️ Incentive Rate
        • High water mark highest value reported (Protect clients from paying twice for the same performance)
        • “2 and 20” means 2% management fee and 20% incentive fee for hedge
        • Fees, notice and lockup periods are negotiable with potential investors.
  • Private Equity (PE)
    • Investing in privately owned companies or in public companies with the intent to take them private.
    • Strategies
      • Leveraged buyouts (LBOs): acquire companies with a significant percentage of the purchase price financed through debt. If debt financing is unavailable or costly, less likely to occur.
        • Characteristics
          • Undervalued/depressed stock price
          • Willing management (Existing management is looking for a deal)
          • Inefficient companies
          • Strong and sustainable cash flow (make interest payments)
          • Low leverage (easier for PE firms to utilize debt)
          • Assets (physical assets can be used as security)
      • Venture capital: invest in private companies with high growth potential.
        • Stages
          • Formative stage:
            • Angel investing: At the idea stage, funds are used to transform the idea into a business plan and to assess market potential. Often provided by individuals.
            • Seed stage: support product development and/or marketing efforts. The first stage at which VC funds invest.
            • Early stage: help companies move toward operation but before commercial production and sales have occurred.
          • Later stage: after commercial production and sales have begun but before any IPO. Funds may be used for expansion.
          • Mezzanine stage: prepare to go public. Represent the bridge between the expanding company and the IPO.
        • Structure (Partnerships where the PE firm is the GP, and investors are LPs)
        • Fee
          • Management fees: based on committed capital.
          • Incentive fees: GP does not earn an incentive fee until the LPs have received initial investment back. (LP first =>GP)
          • Policies that protect LPs (Clawback provision) return any incentive fees until LPs received initial investment back and their profits.
        • Exit strategies
          • Trade Sale: sale of a company to a strategic buyer
          • IPO (BEST)
          • Recapitalization (Pay LP dividend)
          • Secondary Sale:
          • Write-off/Liquidation (WORST)
      • Development capital: minority equity investment in mature firms that are looking for expanding or restructuring opportunities.
      • Distressed investing: buying the debt of mature companies in financial difficulties.
  • Real Estate
    • Performance measurements
      • Appraisal index: use estimates of value (appraisals) / understate volatility
      • Repeat sales (transaction-based) index: sample selection bias
      • REIT index (use the prices of publicly traded shares of REITs to construct) - BEST
        • REIT index and equity returns: high correlation
        • REIT index and bond returns: low correlation
    • Valuation
      • Approach
        • Comparable sales approach
        • Income approach: NOI (Net Operation Income) / Cap rate (Discount rate-growth rate)
        • Cost approach
      • REIT valuation
        • Income approach: Funds from operation (FFO)/Cap rate OR adjusted funds from operation(AFFO)/Cap rate
        • Asset based approach: REIT’s NAV = MKT value of assets – MKT value of liabilities
  • Commodities
    • Relationship between spot prices and expected future prices
      • Contango: Futures price > Spot price , dominated by long hedgers
      • Backwardation: Futures price < Spot price, dominated by short hedgers
    • Sources of return and risk
      • Price return: a long-only investment in commodities derivatives can be positive or negative, depending on the direction of change in the spot price.
      • Collateral yield: an additional return that the investor deposits cash as collateral for the futures contract purchased with the exchange.
      • Roll yield: since commodity derivative contract expire, a speculator or hedger who wants to maintain a position must close out the expiring derivative position and re-establish a new position. “Rolling over” the position leads to gains (backwardation) or losses (contango) are termed the roll yield.
  • Infrastructure
    • Characteristics
      • High barriers to entry
      • Stable cash flows
      • Protection against inflation
      • Better match the longer-term liability structure of some investors
    • Categories
      • Economic vs social infrastructure assets
      • Brownfield vs. greenfield infrastructure investments (老基建/新基建)
    • Forms of Infrastructure Investments MLP
      • Invest directly in the underlying assets
      • Indirect investment vehicles: ETF or MLP (master limited partenership)
    • Risk and Returns
      • Overview Risk depends on underlying asset
      • An inherent risk to many infrastructure investments is regulatory risk
